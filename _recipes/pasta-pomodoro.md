## Ingredients

- 200g spaghetti or rigatoni
- 400g can whole San Marzano tomatoes
- 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil, plus more to finish
- 2 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- ½ tsp fine sea salt, plus more for pasta water
- 1 tsp sugar (optional, to balance acidity)
- Small handful of fresh basil leaves
- Freshly ground black pepper

## Instructions

1. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Salt it aggressively — it should taste like mild seawater. This is your only chance to season the pasta itself.

2. Crush the tomatoes by hand directly into a bowl, discarding any tough cores. Keep all the juices — they become the sauce.

3. Warm the olive oil in a wide, heavy pan over **low heat**. Add the garlic slices and let them gently sizzle for 3–4 minutes until pale golden and fragrant. Do not let them brown or the sauce will taste bitter.

4. Add the crushed tomatoes and their juices in one go — stand back, it will splatter. Add the salt and sugar if using. Stir to combine.

5. Simmer over low heat for 20–25 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the sauce has thickened and the oil has risen back to the surface. This separation is a sign the sauce is ready.

6. While the sauce finishes, cook the pasta until 2 minutes shy of the package instruction — it will finish cooking in the sauce.

7. Using tongs, transfer the pasta directly from the pot into the sauce (a little pasta water going with it is fine — even desirable). Add a splash of pasta cooking water and toss vigorously over medium heat for 1–2 minutes until the pasta is glossy and fully coated.

8. Remove from heat. Tear in the basil, add a generous drizzle of olive oil, and grind over plenty of black pepper. Serve immediately.

## Notes

> **Tomatoes:** San Marzano tomatoes are worth seeking out — their lower acidity and fewer seeds give the cleanest flavour. If unavailable, use the best whole canned tomatoes you can find.
>
> **Oil:** Use a good olive oil. It's not just fat here — it's a main flavour component.
>
> **Scaling:** This sauce doubles and triples easily. It also freezes well for up to 2 months.
